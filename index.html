The Current State of Education

In an age increasingly defined by artificial intelligence, technological acceleration, a culture of achievement, and growing social fragmentation and polarization, education faces a defining challenge: how to nurture what makes us human. Across classrooms and policy agendas, students are prepared to compete, achieve, and succeed—often framed in terms of “college, career, and success.” While these aims have value, their dominance has tilted education toward the outer dimensions of life—performance, productivity, and measurable outcomes—at the expense of the inner dimensions that connect students to their own lives and give those pursuits meaning.

We are living through a broad crisis of connection—reflected in rising anxiety, isolation, and polarization—that points to a fundamental gap in how education has approached human development. We have not cultivated the inner capacities that allow individuals to relate authentically to themselves, to others, and to the world.

Although many social-emotional learning (SEL) and related programs seek to address wellbeing, they often do so by focusing on parts—skills, traits, or competencies—without engaging the underlying wholeness that is the foundation of health and development for both individuals and society.

We lack a shared language or framework for addressing wholeness directly, so efforts to foster wellbeing frequently remain fragmented. Yet wholeness is not abstract or “soft.” It can be approached concretely through creating outlets for students to develop their connection with self, others, and life—connections that integrate the inner and outer dimensions of learning and give coherence to their experience. SEL skills and competencies no doubt have importance, but they need to be anchored in the connectivity that engages their whole self, otherwise students will experience those skills and competencies as fragmented parts that do not interrelate with the core of who they are.

How Did We Get Here?

It’s easy to blame the rapid advance of technology for an intensifying crisis of disconnection and mental health in our youth. While technological advances and social media have been profound accelerants, complicating the journey of connection with self, others, and life, a host of other forces have combined to bring us to this point. To fully appreciate what our youth are dealing with, it is helpful to consider the historical and cultural context in which today's adolescents are growing up.

A few generations prior, society would dictate and hand us an understanding and roadmap of “reality.” Whether commendable or lamentable, this "map" outlined what it meant to be a boy, a girl, a man, a woman, what was expected of us, and what our future held for us. Although these cultural narratives imposed limitations, they also offered a sense of certainty and stability.

Today, these prescribed narratives no longer hold the same weight and certainty in defining who we are and our direction in life. As a result, adolescents are largely left to their own devices to understand themselves, life, and their place in it. This shift has left many youth feeling lost, confused, and in the dark, without the means needed to chart a path to their future. Consequently, teenagers grapple with intense feelings of disconnection, anxiety, and isolation, struggling to find their way in a world that seems increasingly chaotic and uncertain.

Atop these challenges, youth are bombarded with images and messages that fuel concerns about self-image and drive them to project inauthentic versions of themselves on social media and in their lives. The struggle for authenticity is further complicated by a growing distrust in media's portrayal of facts and events, leaving young people uncertain about what information they can rely on as they form their identities and worldviews.

The rapid advancement of AI technology is set to amplify these challenges exponentially. As AI-generated content becomes more sophisticated, we will face increasing difficulty in distinguishing between genuine human expression and artificial creations. This blurring of lines will further complicate teens’ journey toward authentic connection with themselves, others, and the world around them.

In this new landscape, the role of parents and educators is crucial, especially during the pivotal transition from adolescence to independent adulthood. Our most promising solution lies in providing teens with opportunities to develop an internal compass that helps to ground them as they find and chart their unique path. This guidance from within can replace the outdated scripts of the past, enabling young people to uncover their authentic selves and determine their own directions in life.

In this way, we not only address the immediate mental health crisis but also empower the next generation to thrive in an increasingly complex and rapidly changing world.

“The biggest problem growing up today is not actually stress; it’s meaninglessness.” —William Damon, Stanford University

Creating a new Interdisciplinary field of study

This initiative seeks to place “What Makes Us Human?” at the center of educational thought and practice. By supporting educators, researchers, and leaders to explore and advance a pedagogy of connectedness—one that engages students’ whole selves—we can counter the prevailing pedagogy that atomizes the self into discrete parts: academic, social, emotional, behavioral. A pedagogy of connectedness reorients learning around human agency and authentic relationship rather than control, conformity, or performance alone.

Anchoring the many promising strands of educational innovation—identity development, purpose learning, character education, agency and social-emotional learning—in the deeper foundation of what makes us human creates the possibility of moving beyond fixed and externally imposed definitions of success. It allows us to cultivate the inner ground from which real growth emerges: the ability to relate meaningfully to one’s own experience, to others, and to life itself.

Connection is not an abstract ideal; it is a concrete developmental process that can be fostered in any context—from classroom learning to athletics, from community engagement to higher education. When students consciously explore how they relate to their experience, to others, and to life, they strengthen the ethical, emotional, and existential core that sustains resilience, empathy, and purpose.

We aim to deepen the understanding of the “whole child”—not as a collection of parts to be supported separately, but as an integrated, relational individual whose wellbeing depends on a foundation of connectedness to self, others, and life. Wholeness is a byproduct of connectedness; it is foundational to human development—it is what grounds us in our humanity and differentiates us from AI.

By galvanizing a community of educational leaders across disciplines, we aim to collectively lay the foundations for a new interdisciplinary field of study, expanding the narrative of education beyond college and career readiness toward preparedness for life, for relationship, and for the shared human journey. Addressing disconnection at its roots is essential not only for individual wellbeing but also for the flourishing and cohesion of society itself.

Conceptual Framework

Towards a Pedagogy of Wholeness

Wholeness and Connection: Wholeness is the foundation for individual and societal well-being, yet existing programs often address aspects or parts rather than the underlying wholeness. This new interdisciplinary field of study seeks to redefine the whole child as an integrated, relational being. At the heart of this field building approach lies a guiding framework designed to expand our understanding of the “whole child". It centers on three interconnected domains: Connection with Self, Connection with Others, and Connection with Life. Each domain of connection is animated by agency—the capacity to consciously and intentionally engage with and express our experience in relation to ourselves, others, and life. This field situates connection as the generative core of human wholeness that forms the foundation for personal flourishing and collective health.

1. Connection with Self

This domain cultivates the agency to explore and express who we are. Central to this connection is students being able to explore how they relate to and make sense of their experience. This connection fosters authenticity and grounds students in the conviction required to act from their inner compass rather than external validation.

2. Connection with Others

This domain cultivates the agency to form intimate and trusting relationships. Central to this connection is creating opportunities for students to truly see and connect with one another as human beings. This dimension is crucial for reducing polarization and restoring a sense of shared humanity across differences.

3. Connection with Life

This domain cultivates the agency to embrace life with purpose and possibility by supporting students to make sense of their lives, their future, and their place in the world and their communities. This connection nurtures character, authentic citizenship and appreciation for the shared human journey.

Agency and Humanity: Agency and humanity are inextricably linked. The capacity to consciously shape how we relate to ourselves, others, and life is unique to humanity; it differentiates us from both animals and AI. Supporting students in reflecting intentionally on how they relate to their experience—through reflection, relational practice, and authentic meaning-making—whether in collaboration or conflict, profoundly deepens their sense of connectedness. When students are supported in developing this agency, they begin to see themselves not merely as participants in life’s unfolding but as co-authors.

Field Building Strategy

Prioritizing what makes us human is a comprehensive, collaborative, and interdisciplinary approach to forming a shared vision that requires both conceptual clarity and structural coordination. The work unfolds through five interdependent phases:

  1. Collaborate with and convene an interdisciplinary working group of educators, researchers, practitioners, and thought leaders to co-create shared language, intellectual coherence, and long-term stewardship for the field. The centerpiece of this strategy is building a trusted and aligned steering committee, culminating in an initial convening on June 16–18, 2026. Approximately 20 key stakeholders representing research institutions, innovative programs, media, and foundations will gather at the Fetzer Institute in Kalamazoo, Michigan. This convening will serve as the first step to formalize shared language and intention, identify priority areas for collaboration, and build momentum for broader coalition development and next steps in shaping the field. Potential participants/advisors will include Researchers; Mental Health experts; High school Educators and students; Program Leaders/Weavers (bringing theory and practice together); Network of Organizations; Foundations; Government Officials; Journalists.
  2. Refine the framework to clearly convey what it means to prioritize What Makes Us Human means, why it matters, and how it functions as a foundation for human development and societal wellbeing.
  3. Co-Develop core pedagogical principles to highlight best practices, and identify misconceptions to ensure rigor of application – clarifying both effective approaches and common practices that can unintentionally undermine human development.
  4. Demonstrate and communicate the foundational relevance of prioritizing students’ humanity across multiple domains of learning and growth to bring light to the impact possible. Examples include: Character Development/Ethics, Mental Health, SEL, Identity development, Polarization, Agency development, Purpose development, Belonging and Connectedness. Collaborate with experts across domains to align terminology, frameworks, pedagogical principles, and measures; and publish findings and guidance through accessible articles, podcasts, conferences and open-source resources to reach diverse audiences.
  5. Catalyze a broader movement to shift educational narratives, priorities, and investments from an exclusive focus on performance and outcomes toward cultivating human connection, agency, and flourishing in an age of AI. This includes coalition development to unite key stakeholders around the mission; a coordinated strategy for movement building that centers collective ownership and collaboration; thought leadership through conferences, philanthropic convenings, media opportunities, and writing projects focused on collaborative messaging; and a plan to inform and influence public policy later in the process.

Goals for the first Convening (June 16–18)

Convening objectives and outcomes through a community of stewardship

  1. Align around the mission, vision, framework, strategy, approach, and metrics. This convening will serve as a space to test core ideas, surface tensions, and strengthen intellectual coherence.
    Outcome: A shared and coherent narrative for the field-building effort, with key inconsistencies addressed and resolved.
  2. Review and further develop foundational content
    a. Guiding principles for connection with self, others, life
    Outcome: Diverse input to inform a first draft of core pedagogical principles

    b. Common misconceptions and implementation pitfalls related to connection with self, others, and life
    Outcome: Diverse input to inform a first draft of common misconceptions and ineffective practices

    c. Applications across key domains of learning and growth (e.g., mental health, character, purpose, belonging, agency, etc.)
    Outcome: Input across domains to inform a first draft outlining how prioritizing What Makes Us Human applies to different areas of development
  3. Heather Malin will present a synthesis of more than a dozen existing frameworks focused on students’ inner development, enabling participants to identify areas of convergence, clarify core priorities, and explore how disparate approaches can be integrated into a coherent field
  4. Explore inroads for this work in broader culture and across key domains of learning and growth
    Outcome: A phased and prioritized set of strategies for integrating What Makes Us Human into education and related domains over time
  5. Build a coalition to galvanize key stakeholders across education, academia, philanthropy, media, and policy
    Outcome: Clear commitments from participants on how they will contribute to the initiative
    Outcome: A curated list of additional potential early contributors and allies for the movement
  6. Define next steps
    Outcome: Agreement on short term and mid-term priorities and working groups to advance the next phase of field building

Rather than presuming a single, predetermined trajectory, this convening is designed as a space for collective discernment—examining where there is the greatest alignment, readiness, leverage, and need—and to clarify which directions make the most sense to prioritize at this stage of field building. A core goal of the convening is to surface shared insight about how this work can most meaningfully grow, spread, and be stewarded in ways that are authentic, rigorous, and responsive to the current educational and cultural landscape.

Role of Convening Participant

  1. We want you here for your experience and perspective as we embark upon creating this new field to strengthen the overall approach and contribute to a new interdisciplinary field of study.
  2. This is an invitation to a community of Stewardship for a nascent field
  3. Theoretical framework that anchors what makes us uniquely human
  4. Further Develop a strategy to co-create and expand this nascent field

Our collective journey as we enter an age of AI calls for an evolution in education’s purpose—from information transmission to human integration.

Its ultimate aim is to create a lasting educational paradigm that honors the uniqueness of being human and cultivates the inner- and inter-connectedness that technology cannot replicate.

By addressing the underlying crisis of disconnection and Prioritizing What Makes Us Human, we prepare students for meaning, flourishing, and wise coexistence with technology and nature. This educational shift frames connection as both preventive (reducing violence and conflict) and promotive (leading to a healthy society).

Join us in re-envisioning education—to nurture the conscious, connected, and creative capacities that foster exploration of what it means to be human.
